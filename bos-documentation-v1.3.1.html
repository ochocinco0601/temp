Business Observability Data Model

Core Principle

Business observability begins with defining success in terms of business health—customer outcomes, financial impact, compliance, and operational efficiency. Technical observability (signals, instrumentation, dashboards) exists only to support that framing, ensuring every metric and indicator ties back to whether the service is delivering its business value.

What This Is

The Business Observability System (BOS) data model is a standardized way to document services that connects business context with technical reality. It captures how services deliver business value and what happens when they fail.

Key Innovation: Instead of just monitoring technical metrics, BOS tracks business outcomes - whether customers can complete transactions, revenue is flowing, and compliance requirements are met.

The 5-Table Structure

The model uses 52 fields across 5 normalized CSV tables:

1. Services (10 fields)

Core service information including business purpose, criticality tier, and the key performance question. Example: "Treasury Order Funding Service - Executes wire transfers to complete home purchase transactions"

2. SLI Definitions (16 fields)

Defines what to measure and how. Includes both business language (Product Owner) and technical implementation (Developer). Two Types of Metrics:
  • Ratio Metrics: "What percentage of X succeeded?" (e.g., wire transfer success rate)
  • Threshold Metrics: "Is X fast/small/recent enough?" (e.g., response time < 2 seconds)

3. SLO Configurations (10 fields)

Sets targets, thresholds, and measurement windows. Example: "99.5% success rate measured over rolling 7-day window"

4. Impact Assessments (12 fields)

Quantifies business consequences across four categories:
  • Customer Experience: Who is affected and how
  • Financial: Revenue or cost impact
  • Legal/Risk: Compliance and regulatory implications
  • Operational: Internal efficiency impact

5. Operational Metadata (14 fields)

Tracks dashboards, runbooks, alert configurations, and lifecycle management.

What It Produces

From these CSV files, organizations can automatically generate:

  • Business dashboards showing service health in business terms
  • Impact assessments when services degrade or fail
  • SLO tracking with clear business context
  • Standardized alerting tied to business outcomes

Getting Started

  • Choose a critical service - Start with something important but well-understood
  • Fill out the templates - Begin with services.csv, then work through the others
  • Partner across roles - Product Owners, Developers, and Operations each own different fields
  • Iterate and refine - The first version won't be perfect

    • Why This Matters

    Traditional monitoring tells you a service is down. Business Observability tells you:

    • Which customers can't complete their transactions
    • How much revenue is at risk
    • Whether you're meeting regulatory requirements
    • Which business processes are impacted

    This shifts the conversation from "the server is at 90% CPU" to "home purchases are being delayed."

    Design Principles

  • Business First: Every technical metric ties to a business outcome
  • Role-Based: Different personas own different parts of the data
  • Walking Skeleton: Start simple, iterate toward completeness
  • Enterprise Scale: Designed to handle 1000+ services

    • Current Limitations

    This is v1.0 with intentional simplifications:

    • One SLI per service (multi-SLI support coming)
    • No service dependency tracking (v1.2)
    • No organizational hierarchy (v2.0)
    • Limited to basic impact categories

    These limitations are by design - proving the core model before adding complexity.

    Technical Schema Overview

    Semantic Flow Architecture

    The BOS data model implements this semantic flow:

    [Stakeholder] → has an → [Expectation]
     ↓                        ↓
[services.csv]        [slo_configurations.csv]
     ↓
...which, if broken, causes an → [Impact]
     ↓
[impact_assessments.csv]
     ↓
...which is detected by → [Telemetry]
     ↓
[sli_definitions.csv]
     ↓
...which creates an actionable → [Signal]
     ↓
[operational_metadata.csv]
     ↓
...which informs both a → [Playbook] and a [Dashboard]

    This flow drives why we have 5 connected tables, each capturing a stage in this chain.

    Table Relationships

    Primary Table: services

    • Key: service_id (unique identifier)
    • Purpose: Core service definition and business context
    • Cardinality: One row per service

    Dependent Tables (all link via service_id)

    • sli_definitions: One row per service (1:1)
    • slo_configurations: One row per service (1:1)
    • impact_assessments: 1-4 rows per service (1:many)
    - One row per impact category (customer, financial, legal, operational)
    • operational_metadata: One row per service (1:1)

    Query Patterns

    Common queries your platform will need to support:

    Basic Service Queries

    Get complete service data
    SELECT * FROM services s
LEFT JOIN sli_definitions sli ON s.service_id = sli.service_id
LEFT JOIN slo_configurations slo ON s.service_id = slo.service_id
LEFT JOIN impact_assessments ia ON s.service_id = ia.service_id
LEFT JOIN operational_metadata om ON s.service_id = om.service_id
WHERE s.service_id = 'SVC001';
    Find critical services by tier
    SELECT * FROM services
WHERE tierLevel <= 2
ORDER BY tierLevel, serviceName;

    Specialized Query Patterns

    Find validation and compliance services
    SELECT * FROM services
WHERE businessPurpose LIKE '%validat%'
   OR businessPurpose LIKE '%monitor%'
   OR businessPurpose LIKE '%compliance%';
    Get zero-tolerance services
    SELECT s.*, slo.sloTarget, slo.sloTargetRationale
FROM services s
JOIN slo_configurations slo ON s.service_id = slo.service_id
WHERE slo.sloTarget = 100.0;  -- Zero tolerance services
    Find services with exception queues
    SELECT s.*, sli.thresholdValue, sli.thresholdOperator
FROM services s
JOIN sli_definitions sli ON s.service_id = sli.service_id
WHERE sli.goodEventsCriteria_Dev LIKE '%exception_queue%'
   OR sli.goodEventsCriteria_Dev LIKE '%manual_intervention%';

    Impact Analysis Queries

    Get all impacts for a service
    SELECT * FROM impact_assessments
WHERE service_id = 'SVC001'
ORDER BY impactCategory;
    Find services with regulatory risk
    `sql SELECT DISTINCT s.*, ia.regulatoryImpact FROM services s JOIN impact_assessments ia ON s.service_id = ia.service_id WHERE ia.impactCategory = 'legal_risk' AND ia.regulatoryImpact IS NOT NULL;

    Common Query Patterns for Different Service Purposes

    Exception Queue Monitoring

    For services tracking items requiring manual review:
    • Track percentage in exception queue vs total processed
    • Threshold typically 5-10% depending on complexity
    • Query pattern: WHERE manual_intervention=false

    Compliance Validation

    For services verifying policy adherence:
    • Often have 100% SLO targets (zero tolerance)
    • Check for violations rather than successes
    • Query pattern: WHERE policy_violation=false

    Completeness Tracking

    For services finding missing transactions:
    • Compare what should exist vs what does exist
    • Often involves JOIN queries across systems
    • Query pattern: WHERE expected=true AND processed=false

    Data Integrity Rules

  • service_id must exist in services table before adding to other tables
  • impactCategory must be one of: customer_experience, financial, legal_risk, operational
  • sliType must be either:
    • - ratioMetric: Percentage-based (goodEvents/totalEvents) - thresholdMetric: Boundary-based (value < threshold)
  • serviceType must be one of: customer-facing, internal, infrastructure
  • status must be one of: draft, active, deprecated
  • financialImpact: Must be actual transaction amounts, not hypothetical costs

    • CSV to Database Mapping

    Simple approach for initial implementation:

  • Read all CSV files
  • Create tables with all fields as VARCHAR/TEXT
  • Add indexes on service_id for all tables
  • Add unique constraint on services.service_id
  • Implement foreign key constraints

    • Performance Considerations

    For 1000+ services scale:

    • Index on service_id (all tables)
    • Index on tierLevel (services)
    • Index on status (operational_metadata)
    • Consider partitioning impact_assessments by impactCategory

    Implementation Notes

    • Start with direct CSV import for walking skeleton
    • Add validation layer after basic functionality works
    • Consider caching layer for read-heavy operations
  • Plan for versioning strategy early (version field exists)

    • Known Gaps and Limitations

    Intentional Omissions for v1.0

    These gaps are by design for the walking skeleton approach. Do not implement these yet.

    1. Service Dependencies ❌

    Not Tracked: No upstream/downstream service relationships
    • Cannot map service call chains
    • Cannot trace failure propagation
    • Cannot identify critical path dependencies
    Why Omitted: Complexity of many-to-many relationships; need discovery methodology first Future Plan (v1.2): Add dependencies.csv with source/target/criticality fields

    2. Organizational Hierarchy ❌

    Not Included: No L3/L4 business unit structure
    • Cannot roll up metrics by organization
    • Cannot map to executive reporting structure
    • No matrix organization support
    Why Omitted: Every enterprise has unique org structure; need input first Future Plan (v2.0): Add organizational dimension table

    3. Business Functions ❌

    Not Modeled: No connection to business processes
    • Cannot answer "what business function does this support?"
    • Missing business capability taxonomy
    • No process flow mapping
    Why Omitted: Requires business architecture maturity that doesn't exist yet Future Plan: Add business_functions.csv when process taxonomy defined

    4. Multiple SLIs per Service ❌

    Current Limit: One SLI per service only
    • Real services often have multiple SLIs
    • Cannot track different aspects (latency + availability)
    • Oversimplified for complex services
    Why Limited: Proving pattern with simplest case first Future Plan (v1.1): Extend to many-to-one relationship

    5. Technical Service Layer ❌

    Not Separated: No distinction between business and technical services
    • Cannot model microservice architectures
    • Missing container/pod level details
    • No deployment topology
    Why Omitted: Focus on business observability first, technical details second

    6. Time-based Variations ❌

    Not Supported: No concept of different SLOs by time
    • Cannot set different targets for business hours
    • No seasonal adjustment capability
    • Single static target only
    Why Omitted: Advanced feature, not needed for initial proof

    Implementation Guidance

    What This Means for Platform Design

  • Design for Extension: These gaps will be filled in future versions
  • Don't Over-Engineer: Resist adding these features proactively
  • Feedback Loop: Use v1.0 to discover real requirements
  • Schema Evolution: Plan for non-breaking additions

    • Migration Strategy

    When these gaps are addressed:

    • New tables will be added (not modifications to existing)
    • Foreign key relationships will extend current model
    • Backward compatibility will be maintained
    • Optional fields pattern for gradual adoption

    Product Owners

    Quick Start Guide for Product Owners

    Your Role in BOS

    As a Product Owner, you define the business context for your service. You answer: What does this service do for the business? What happens when it fails? Who is affected?

    The Validation Chain

    A problem is only solved if the intended outcome is achieved. Achieving an outcome requires knowing what that outcome is. Knowing if the outcome was achieved requires explicit validation. Validation requires instrumentation and measurement. Instrumentation requires the goal to be defined as a requirement in the first place.

    Therefore: If we want to know whether a business problem is solved, we must treat the business goal as a requirement and build in validation through SLIs and SLOs.

    The Three Tables You Own

    1. services.csv - Core Service Information

    You own all 10 fields in this table:

    		Field What to Provide Example
    service_id Unique identifier (work with tech team) SVC001
    serviceName Technical name of service treasury-order-funding-service
    displayName Human-friendly name Treasury Order Funding Service
    businessPurpose What it does in business terms Execute wire transfers for home purchases
    serviceType customer-facing, internal, or infrastructure customer-facing
    tierLevel 1 (critical) to 6 (low impact) 1
    businessUnit Your organization Home Lending
    performanceQuestion The ONE question that matters What % of wires complete on time?
    tags Optional categorization mortgage,funding
    productOwner Your email sarah.chen@company.com

    2. sli_definitions.csv - Defining Success (Your Fields)

    You define success in business language - developers will translate to technical:

    		Field What to Provide Example
    goodEventsCriteria_PO What counts as success? Wire transfer completed successfully
    totalEventsCriteria_PO What are we measuring? All wire transfer attempts

    Key Point: Describe this as you would to an executive, not an engineer.

    3. impact_assessments.csv - What Happens When Things Fail

    Create 1-4 rows per service, one for each applicable impact type:

    		Impact Type What to Document Example
    customer_experience Who can't do what 850 homebuyers can't close on their homes
    financial Actual transaction dollars flowing through service $323M daily wire transfer volume
    legal_risk Regulatory violations, compliance breaches TRID timing violations, CFPB penalties
    operational Manual work, efficiency loss 10 ops staff handle escalations

    Important: Financial impact captures real money processed by your service (wire transfers, loans, trades, fees), NOT hypothetical costs like "$10K/hour downtime".

    Step-by-Step Process

    Step 1: Start with WHY

    Before filling any fields, answer:
    • Why does this service exist?
    • What business problem does it solve?
    • Who relies on it?

    Step 2: Fill services.csv

    Start here - this is your service's identity card.

    Step 3: Define Success

    In sli_definitions.csv, describe in plain language:
    • What does "working correctly" mean?
    • What percentage of the time should it work?

    Step 4: Document Impact

    Think through what breaks when your service fails:
    • Customers: Can they complete their transaction?
    • Revenue: Is money at risk?
    • Compliance: Any regulatory issues?
    • Operations: Who has to scramble?

    Step 5: Partner with Tech Teams

    • Developers will translate your business definitions to technical queries
    • Operations will add monitoring and runbook details
    • Stay involved to ensure business context isn't lost

    • SLO Target: 95% posting accuracy

    • Example Walkthrough: Treasury Order Funding Service

    Service Context

    This example shows how to document a wire transfer service that enables home purchases. It's a Tier 1 (critical) service because failures directly impact customers' ability to close on their homes.

    Step 1: Core Service Definition (services.csv)

    service_id: SVC001
serviceName: treasury-order-funding-service
displayName: Treasury Order Funding Service
businessPurpose: Execute wire transfers to complete home purchase transactions
serviceType: customer-facing
tierLevel: 1
businessUnit: Home Lending
performanceQuestion: What percentage of wire transfers complete successfully on scheduled closing date?
tags: mortgage,funding,critical
productOwner: sarah.chen@company.com

    Why These Choices:

    • tierLevel = 1: Home closings are time-critical with legal implications
    • customer-facing: Directly impacts external customers (homebuyers)
    • performanceQuestion: Focuses on the business outcome (successful closing), not technical metrics

    Step 2: Defining Success (sli_definitions.csv)

    Business Language (Product Owner fills these):

    goodEventsCriteria_PO: Wire transfer completed successfully enabling scheduled closing
totalEventsCriteria_PO: All wire transfer attempts for scheduled closings

    Technical Translation (Developer fills these):

    goodEventsCriteria_Dev: status='FUNDED' AND closing_date=scheduled_date AND amount>0
totalEventsCriteria_Dev: request_type='CLOSING_FUNDING' AND scheduled_date IS NOT NULL

    Why This Matters:

    The PO defines success as "closing happened on time" while the developer translates this to specific database fields and conditions.

    Step 3: Setting Targets (slo_configurations.csv)

    sloTarget: 99.5
sloTargetRationale: Based on commitment to real estate partners and regulatory requirements
timeWindow: 7d
timeWindowType: rolling

    Business Reasoning:

    • 99.5% target: Allows ~4 failures per 1000 transactions
    • 7-day rolling: Matches weekly business reporting cycles
    • Rationale: Not arbitrary - tied to partner agreements

    Step 4: Impact Assessment (impact_assessments.csv)

    Every service can have up to FOUR types of impact. This service affects three of the four categories, so it has THREE rows:

    Row 1: Customer Impact

    service_id: SVC001
impactCategory: customer_experience
stakeholderType: homebuyers
stakeholderCount: 850 daily
failureScenario: Wire transfer fails preventing scheduled closing
businessConsequence: Homebuyers cannot complete purchase and may lose rate lock

    Row 2: Financial Impact

    service_id: SVC001
impactCategory: financial
stakeholderType: company
failureScenario: Wire transfer failures prevent closings
businessConsequence: Lost transaction revenue and potential customer churn
financialImpact: $323M daily wire volume
    Note: The $323M represents actual money flowing through the service daily - real wire transfers that won't be processed if the service fails. This is NOT a hypothetical "cost of downtime" calculation.

    Row 3: Operational Impact

    service_id: SVC001
impactCategory: operational
stakeholderType: operations team
stakeholderCount: 10 people
failureScenario: Wire failures require manual intervention
businessConsequence: Manual escalation process consuming ops capacity

    Why No Legal/Risk Impact?

    This service doesn't have specific regulatory timing requirements (like TRID disclosures) or compliance obligations that would be violated by a failure. Standard banking regulations apply but aren't at risk from service failures. When Legal/Risk WOULD Apply:
    • TRID disclosure services (3-day/7-day timing requirements)
    • BSA/AML transaction monitoring (must review within timeframes)
    • Fair lending decision services (must comply with ECOA timelines)
    • Data privacy services (CCPA/GDPR requirements)

    Step 5: Operational Details (operational_metadata.csv)

    alertNotificationTargets: email:homelending-ops@company.com;pagerduty:PD-HL-001
dashboardUrl: https://grafana.company.com/d/funding-slo
runbookUrl: https://wiki.company.com/runbooks/funding
alertingConfigured: true
status: active

    Key Lessons from This Example

    1. Start with Business Purpose

    Everything flows from understanding why this service matters to the business.

    2. Multiple Impacts Are Common

    Most critical services affect customers, revenue, AND operations.

    3. Quantify Where Possible

    • 850 customers daily (specific)
    • $380,000 average transaction (measurable)
    • 10 ops people (countable)

    4. Business vs Technical Language

    Notice how PO fields use business terms while Dev fields use technical syntax.

    5. Not Every Field Applies

    • No legal_risk impact for this service
    • Some optional fields left empty
    • That's perfectly fine

    Applying This to Your Service

    Ask yourself:

  • What's my service's core business purpose?
  • How do I define success in business terms?
  • Who suffers when it fails? (customers, revenue, compliance, operations)
  • What's a reasonable success target based on business needs?
  • Who needs to be alerted when something goes wrong?

    • Remember

    This is a walking skeleton - start simple:

    • Document one service well rather than many poorly
    • Focus on the most critical business impacts
    • Iterate based on what you learn
  • Perfect is the enemy of good enough
