Business Observability for IT Operations

An operating approach for detecting, responding to, and restoring business service

Working Draft

1. The Landscape

Effective IT operations for monitoring and observability requires many different kinds of capability — from defining what success looks like to learning from what goes wrong. Before narrowing to any specific focus, here is how that full landscape organizes.

These six domains represent fundamentally different kinds of work. They are not steps in a process or layers in a stack — they are categories. Each contains specific capabilities that organizations invest in and maintain.

Four of six domains, in a natural sequence. Intent is the prerequisite — you cannot detect what you haven’t defined. The deliverable is detect, respond, restore.

Our incident response machinery is mature and repeatable. Our ability to answer “what is the business experiencing during those incidents?” is not. Some teams have built this capability — bespoke solutions that don’t transfer. The gap isn’t that no one has solved this. It’s that we can’t scale what works.

How to Read This Map

  • App Dev Manager: “If my team hasn’t delivered these queries, Platform is blocked.”

Dependencies flow left to right: Platform cannot build dashboards without App Dev queries. App Dev cannot write queries without Product intent. Product cannot validate without operational evidence.

Without Product defining business success criteria, monitoring stays technically focused. Without App Dev implementing business signals, the gap between context and telemetry can’t be closed. Without Platform sustaining the monitoring layer, insights don’t persist. Each director’s teams play a role.

4. What It Looks Like

The responsibility map above shows what each team delivers. Here is what that work actually looks like — six activities, two per knowledge domain, showing the path from business context through measurement to operational evidence.

These activities are organized by the knowledge they require — business context, technical implementation, operational monitoring. In many teams, one experienced person holds multiple perspectives. The knowledge is required; the staffing model is flexible.

Each activity produces a named output that downstream work depends on. Read from Product through Dev to Platform to see the full chain.

Product
Define What Matters
Capture who depends on the service, what they expect from it, and what happens to the business when those expectations aren’t met. For each stakeholder group: a specific expectation (“credit checks complete within 5 seconds during business hours”), a concrete business impact (“loan applicants wait, application abandonment increases, $2.3M daily revenue at risk”), and an impact category — customer experience, financial, legal/regulatory, or operational.
Produces
Stakeholder Expectations Set — 2–4 cards per service, each connecting a stakeholder to an expectation to a business consequence.
Enables
Dev to understand what “success” means when writing queries. Platform to surface business context on dashboards and in alert messages.
Define Business Signals
Specify what to measure — health signals (“are we meeting expectations?”) and impact signals (“how bad is it when we fail?”). All definitions use business language: “Wire Transfer Success Rate” not “FUNDING_STATUS query rate.” The technical implementation is a separate concern — this activity defines WHAT matters, not HOW to measure it.
Produces
Business Signal Set — 2–5 health signals and 1–3 impact signals per service, each with a business-friendly name and description.
Enables
Dev to write queries that implement each signal’s measurement. Platform to name dashboard panels and alert conditions in business terms.
App Dev
Assess Data Availability
Identify where data lives for each signal — which platform (Splunk, database, Prometheus), which specific source (index, table, metric), and whether the data is accessible and sufficient. This is the bridge between “what the business wants measured” and “what can actually be measured.” When data doesn’t exist, the assessment documents the gap and flags it for instrumentation work.
Produces
Data Source Assessment — a record per signal with platform and connection details, or a documented gap.
Enables
Query writing — you cannot write a query without knowing which table or index to query against.
Write the Queries
Translate business signal definitions into executable queries — turning “credit checks complete within 5 seconds” into SQL or Splunk that counts good events versus total events, or measures a value against a threshold. This is the implementation side of the business requirement: the business defined WHAT to measure, the developer writes HOW to measure it.
Produces
Signal Query Set — production-ready queries for each signal, validated against real data.
Enables
Platform to build dashboards showing health status and configure alerting against measurable thresholds.
Platform
Build Dashboards
Assemble upstream definitions into a dashboard that lets a non-technical stakeholder answer “Is this service meeting its business commitments?” within seconds. Three sections in a deliberate order: business context (what is this service and who depends on it?), business health (is it meeting its targets?), business impact (what is the consequence when it isn’t?).
Produces
Executive Business Service Dashboard — context panel, health signals vs. targets, quantified business impact across four categories.
Enables
Executives and incident responders to assess business service health during incidents and Start-of-Day reviews — without translating technical metrics.
Configure Alerting
Define when alerts fire and who gets notified — setting warning and critical thresholds per signal, routing notifications to the right people, and writing alert messages that carry business context. Responders see “850 homebuyers cannot close on scheduled date” not just “threshold breached.”
Produces
Alert Configuration — thresholds, notification routing, and business-context alert messages per service.
Enables
On-call responders to triage with business context — knowing who is affected and how badly before opening a technical dashboard.

Pattern, Not Exhaustive List

These six activities are representative. The full set follows the same structure: named activity, specific output, downstream dependency. What matters is the shape — business context flows through measurement into operational evidence, with each step producing something the next step requires.

5. The Path Forward

Start by identifying 2–3 critical business flows per product area and capturing their business context — who depends on them, what they expect, what happens when expectations aren’t met. This is the work that requires your teams’ business knowledge. Instrumentation and operational artifacts follow naturally once that context exists.

Flow selection is joint work. Platform and each product area identify candidates together — where there’s business importance and a team ready to engage. No single criterion determines which flows to start with; the selection itself is the first collaborative act.

One Flow, End to End

Consider the Credit Check Service. Define What Matters captures that loan applicants expect credit decisions within 5 seconds and that $2.3M daily revenue is at risk when they don’t get them. Define Business Signals specifies the measurements — credit check success rate, response time against the threshold. Dev assesses data availability and writes the queries that turn those signals into executable measurements against existing telemetry. Platform builds the dashboard and configures alerting. During the next incident, the team sees immediately: how many applicants are waiting, what revenue is exposed. That context was captured once and is available every time.

The Ask

Each director ensures their senior technology managers and teams are prepared to engage in flow identification and contextualization sessions as Platform coordinates with each product area.

Platform provides the framework, templates, and embedded support. Product areas provide the business context and team engagement. The first sessions are where we start — concrete, scoped, and joint.
