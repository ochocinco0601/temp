Part 1: What is FMEA? (The 3-Minute Overview)

What Problem Does It Solve?

Most production incidents happen at predictable failure points that we could have identified and mitigated before they caused customer impact. FMEA is a structured 2-hour session where your team:

  1. Identifies where your system could fail
  2. Scores the risk of each failure point
  3. Takes action on the top 3 highest-risk items

When to Conduct FMEA

TriggerWhy
New feature or applicationIdentify risks before production (ideally part of architecture review)
After high-severity incidentPrevent recurrence, find related failure points
Major infrastructure changeMigration, new vendor, technology swap introduces new risks
Periodic refreshQuarterly review for critical systems (RTO 0-4)
New client/integrationExternal access points create new failure scenarios

The Lightweight Approach

  • ‚úÖ Two flows per team this quarter (your choice of critical flows)
  • ‚úÖ 2-hour facilitated session (not multi-day effort)
  • ‚úÖ Simple template (8 columns, not 17)
  • ‚úÖ Top 3 actions (30-day deadline for quick wins)
  • ‚úÖ Self-service with optional support
Scope Guidance: Pick End-to-End Flows Focus on flows across tech stacks, not single components:
  • ‚úÖ Good: Client ‚Üí API ‚Üí Database ‚Üí Response
  • ‚úÖ Good: Batch process ‚Üí File transfer ‚Üí Database ‚Üí API exposure
  • ‚ùå Avoid: Single API analysis or single process analysis

Expected Outcomes

  • Tangible improvements delivered (alerts, runbooks, circuit breakers, contingencies)
  • 3-5 actionable items with owners and deadlines
  • Documented failure points for faster incident response
  • Team alignment on highest-risk areas

Why Improvements Matter (Outcome Mapping)

Improvement TypeHow It Drives Business Outcomes
AlertFaster detection ‚Üí Lower TTR
RunbookFaster diagnosis ‚Üí Lower TTR, less human intervention
Circuit BreakerAuto-recovery ‚Üí Fewer incidents, lower customer impact
ContingencyManual fallback ‚Üí Lower customer impact during outages
ObservabilityFaster root cause ‚Üí Lower TTR

Part 2: How to Run a 2-Hour FMEA Session

Before the Meeting (3 Days Prior)

Send to all attendees:

  • Link to Rapid FMEA Template (Excel file)
  • Link to this FMEA Guide
  • Meeting agenda
  • Pre-work: "Come prepared to discuss: What are the 5 most likely failure points in [your flow]?"

Who to invite (5-8 people):

  • Product Owner (business context)
  • Tech Lead (architecture knowledge)
  • SRE/Operations (incident history)
  • Developer (code/integration details)
  • Optional: Vendor rep (if relevant to the flow)

During the Meeting (2 Hours)

Segment 1: Introduction (15 minutes)

  • Facilitator explains FMEA purpose and Rapid Template
  • Team identifies the scope: Which flow/system are we analyzing?
  • Draw simple diagram: "What are the major components in this flow?"

Segment 2: Identify Failure Points (60 minutes)

Key Focus Areas Checklist:

Focus AreaWhat to ExamineCheck
External DependenciesVendor APIs, third-party services, downstream systems‚òê
Data QualityValidation errors, missing fields, format issues, corruption‚òê
Capacity/ScaleConnection pools, queue depth, throughput limits‚òê
Timeouts & LatencyAPI timeouts, slow queries, network delays‚òê
Batch ProcessingJob failures, partial completion, retry handling‚òê
ConfigurationEnvironment drift, feature flags, secrets expiration‚òê
Human TouchpointsManual steps, approvals, handoffs between teams‚òê
Legacy SystemsMonoliths, outdated tech, undocumented behavior‚òê
SME ConcentrationSingle point of knowledge (what if key person is unavailable?)‚òê
Incident HistoryRepeat failures, known weak points, past outages‚òê
Observability GapsMissing logs, dashboards, alerts for this flow‚òê
Recovery ProceduresAre failover/restart procedures documented and tested?‚òê
Facilitator tip: Walk through each focus area explicitly. Teams often miss Legacy Systems, SME Concentration, and Recovery Procedures.

For each component in your flow, brainstorm:

  • "What could go wrong?" (API timeout, database connection failure, data corruption, etc.)
  • "What's the customer impact?" (transaction fails, incorrect data, delay, etc.)
  • "How likely is it?" (Rate 1-5: 1=rare, 5=happens frequently)
  • "How bad would it be?" (Rate 1-5: 1=minor inconvenience, 5=critical outage)

Goal: Identify 10-15 potential failure points

Segment 3: Prioritize Top 3 (30 minutes)

  • Review RPN (auto-calculated in template: Likelihood x Severity)
  • Sort by highest RPN
  • Select Top 3 failure points to address
  • For each, define one action that would reduce the risk
  • Assign owner and 30-day deadline for each action

Segment 4: Wrap-Up (15 minutes)

  • Summarize Top 3 actions and owners
  • Schedule 30-day follow-up (15 min check-in)
  • Facilitator saves completed template to shared folder
  • Add actions to team backlog

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

  • ‚ùå "Don't analyze everything" ‚Üí Focus on 10-15 most critical failure points
  • ‚ùå "Don't debate scores for 20 minutes" ‚Üí If team is split between 3 and 4, pick the higher number and move on
  • ‚ùå "Don't create vague actions" ‚Üí "Improve monitoring" is bad; "Add Splunk alert for error code XYZ" is good
  • ‚ùå "Don't set unrealistic timelines" ‚Üí If action takes >30 days, break it into smaller first step

Part 4: How to Pick Top 3 Actions (Prioritization Guide)

Step 1: Calculate RPN for All Failure Points

The template auto-calculates: RPN = Likelihood √ó Severity

Step 2: Sort by RPN (Highest First)

Focus on items with RPN ‚â• 12 (High risk)

Step 3: Apply the "30-Day Filter"

For each high-RPN item, ask:

  • "Can we make meaningful progress in 30 days?"
  • ‚úÖ Yes ‚Üí Include in Top 3
  • ‚ùå No (requires 6 months/major investment) ‚Üí Escalate separately, pick next item

Step 4: Balance Quick Wins vs. Impact

Ideal Top 3 mix:

  1. One "easy win" (low effort, fast implementation) ‚Üí Builds momentum
  2. One "medium lift" (requires coordination, 2-3 weeks) ‚Üí Demonstrates commitment
  3. One "high impact" (addresses biggest risk, may take full 30 days) ‚Üí Delivers value

Examples of Good 30-Day Actions

Failure PointGood Action (30 days)Poor Action (avoid)
Database connection pool exhaustion Add Grafana alert for pool utilization >80%, update runbook with scaling steps Redesign entire connection pooling architecture
No business contingency for vendor outage Document manual workaround, train 2 ops team members Build fully automated backup vendor integration
Missing observability for batch job failures Add Splunk alert for job failure, create ServiceNow auto-ticket Implement full batch job orchestration platform

Part 5: 30-Day Follow-Up: Did It Work?

Success Criteria Validation

QuestionHow to Validate
Is the improvement deployed?Check production: alert exists, runbook published, code merged
Is it working as designed?Alert fired correctly in test? Runbook followed by on-call?
Has RPN decreased?Re-score: Is the failure less likely or less severe now?
Evidence of value?Any incidents avoided, faster response, or issues caught?

Re-Scoring RPN

For each completed action:

  1. Re-assess Likelihood - Has the chance of failure decreased?
  2. Re-assess Severity - Would impact be lower now? (e.g., faster detection)
  3. Calculate new RPN
  4. Document % reduction

Quick Reference Card

The 5-Step FMEA Process

  1. Scope: Pick one critical flow to analyze
  2. Identify: List 10-15 potential failure points
  3. Score: Rate likelihood (1-5) and severity (1-5) for each
  4. Prioritize: Focus on Top 3 highest RPN items
  5. Act: Define actions, assign owners, 30-day deadline

Success Formula

  • ‚úÖ Right people in the session (product, tech, SRE, dev)
  • ‚úÖ Focus on failure points, not theory
  • ‚úÖ Don't debate scores‚Äîgo with team gut feel
  • ‚úÖ Be specific in actions (not "improve monitoring")
  • ‚úÖ Track progress and categorize improvements delivered
  • ‚úÖ Point at what exists now that didn't exist before

FAQs

Q: How long should this take?
A: Initial FMEA session: 2 hours. Template fill-out happens during the session.

Q: What if we have more than 15 failure points?
A: Focus on the most critical 10-15. You can always do another FMEA on the same flow later.

Q: What if all our RPNs are low (<10)?
A: Great! That means you have good controls. Document it anyway‚Äîuseful for incident response and onboarding.

Q: What if our Top 3 action takes longer than 30 days?
A: Break it into a smaller first step. Example: Full circuit breaker implementation might take 60 days, but "Add retry logic for one API call" could be done in 30.

FMEA Facilitation Guide

For Session Leaders: How to Run a Successful 2-Hour FMEA

Your Role as Facilitator

Your job is to structure and guide the group through this analysis session. Keep the discussion moving, make sure everyone contributes, and help the group reach decisions.

Your responsibilities:

  • ‚úÖ Keep the session on track (follow the agenda, timebox discussions)
  • ‚úÖ Make sure all voices are heard (bring in quieter people, don't let one person dominate)
  • ‚úÖ Keep scoring practical (push for clarity, move forward when good enough)
  • ‚úÖ Capture ideas in the template as the group discusses
  • ‚úÖ Focus on actionable outcomes (decisions people can commit to and deliver in 30 days)

Pre-Session Checklist (3 Days Before)

1. Confirm Attendees (5-8 people)

Required roles:

  • Product Owner (business context, customer impact)
  • Tech Lead (architecture, design decisions)
  • SRE/Operations (incident history, current alerting)
  • Developer (code, integrations, data flow)

Optional (invite if relevant):

  • Vendor Representative (if third-party integration)
Important: If you're missing product + tech + operations perspectives, the analysis will be incomplete. Reschedule to ensure you have all three dimensions represented.

2. Send Pre-Work Email (Template)

3. Prepare Materials

  • Open Rapid FMEA Template (Excel file)
  • Have Quick Start Guide open for reference
  • Set up screen share (you'll capture notes in template during session)
  • Prepare simple diagram of the flow

During the Session: Minute-by-Minute Guide

Minutes 0-15: Introduction & Scope Definition

Opening (5 min)

Your script:

"Thanks everyone for joining. Our goal is to identify where [Flow Name] could realistically fail and commit to action on the highest-impact items. This is about continuous improvement, not blame‚Äîwe're building on what the team already knows about this system."

"By the end of this session, we'll have identified 10-15 potential failure points and committed to 3 concrete actions with owners and 30-day deadlines."

Define Scope (10 min)

Key principle: Focus on end-to-end functional flows across tech stacks, not single components.

Questions to ask:

  1. "What exactly are we analyzing today?"
    • ‚úÖ Good: "ACH payment processing flow, from customer submission to bank confirmation"
    • ‚ùå Bad: "The entire payment platform" (too broad)
  2. "Can someone draw/show the flow at a high level?"
  3. "What are the boundaries?" (in scope vs out of scope)

Minutes 15-75: Identify & Score Failure Points

Part A: Brainstorm Failure Points (30 min)

Your script:

"Let's walk through the flow component by component. For each part, ask: What could go wrong? Don't filter‚Äîjust capture ideas. We'll prioritize later."

Facilitator technique: "Walk the Flow"

  • Point to first box in diagram: "What could fail here?"
  • Capture each idea as a row in template
  • Move to next component, repeat

Failure node focus areas:

Focus AreaPrompt Questions
External Dependencies"What if [vendor/API] is down or slow?"
Data Quality"What if data is malformed or missing?"
Capacity/Scale"What breaks under 10x load?"
Timeouts & Latency"What if response takes too long?"
Batch Processing"What if the job fails halfway?"
Configuration"What if config is wrong or stale?"
Human Touchpoints"What manual steps could fail?"
Facilitation tips:
  • Push for clarity: If answers stay too generic, probe for specifics
  • Draw out quieter voices: Direct questions to people who haven't spoken much
  • Park tangents: Redirect solution debates: "Let's capture the failure point first"

Part B: Score Likelihood & Severity (30 min)

Your script:

"Now we'll score each failure. Likelihood: How often could this happen (1-5)? Severity: How bad is customer impact (1-5)?"

For EACH row:

  1. Read the Failure Point and Customer Impact
  2. Ask: "What's the likelihood?"
  3. Team discusses briefly (2 min max)
  4. Ask for consensus: "I'm hearing 3 and 4‚Äîlet's say 4 to be safe. Anyone strongly disagree?"
  5. Enter scores, RPN auto-calculates

Minutes 75-105: Prioritize Top 3 Actions

Part A: Sort & Identify High-Risk Items (5 min)

  • Click filter arrow on Column E (RPN)
  • Sort Z‚ÜíA (highest first)
  • Highlight rows with RPN ‚â• 12

Part B: Define Actions for Top 3 (20 min)

For each high-RPN item:

  1. "What ONE action would reduce the risk here?" (5 min discussion)
  2. "Can we complete it in 30 days?"
    • ‚úÖ Yes ‚Üí Capture in template
    • ‚ùå No ‚Üí "What's a smaller first step we can do in 30 days?"

Good action examples:

  • "Add Splunk alert: error code 500 from Hogan API, threshold >10/min"
  • "Update runbook section 3.2 with failover steps"
  • "Implement 3-retry logic with exponential backoff"
  • "Document manual workaround for vendor outage, train 2 ops team members"

Minutes 105-120: Wrap-Up & Next Steps

Summarize (5 min)

Your script:

"Great session! Let's recap what we committed to:"

- Flow analyzed: [Name]
- Failure points identified: [Number]
- Top 3 actions:
  1. [Action] - [Owner] - [Due Date]
  2. [Action] - [Owner] - [Due Date]
  3. [Action] - [Owner] - [Due Date]

Assign Follow-Up Actions (5 min)

  • You (facilitator): Save completed FMEA to shared folder
  • Tech Lead: Create 3 Jira tickets for actions
  • All owners: Accept Jira tickets, add to sprint
  • Product Owner: Schedule 30-day follow-up

Scenarios You May Encounter

Scenario 1: "We don't have time for this"

Reframe:

"I understand‚Äîeveryone's busy. Consider this a 2-hour investment to surface and prevent incidents that typically cost 10+ hours to resolve."

Scenario 2: "Everything feels high-risk"

Approach:

"That's actually valuable insight. Let's apply a practical filter: Of these 10 high-risk items, which 3 can we meaningfully improve in the next 30 days?"

Scenario 3: The group debates a score for 10+ minutes

Move forward:

"I'm hearing good arguments on both sides. Let's go with the conservative [higher] score to be safe‚Äîwe can adjust if actual incident data tells us otherwise."

Scenario 4: Someone gets defensive

Redirect with respect:

"I appreciate you owning this‚Äîyour expertise is exactly why you're here. Every system has failure points; the best teams document them proactively so they can improve."

RPN Calculation (3-Factor)

RPN = Severity x Occurrence x Detection

FactorScaleLow EndHigh End

Note: This differs from the Rapid FMEA template which uses 2-factor (Likelihood x Severity on 1-5 scales). The 3-factor approach adds "Detection" to capture how likely failures will be caught before customer impact.

FMEA Introduction

FMEA methodology is used for identifying failure points in applications. By analyzing systemic points of failure in an application and/or data flow, it helps in risk reduction and helps application/platform teams in prioritizing focus on key failure nodes in case of production incidents.

FMEA helps to:

  • Identify potential failure modes
  • Assess the effects of those failures
  • Determine the causes of failures
  • Prioritize risks
  • Recommend actions to mitigate risk(s)

Benefits of FMEA in Software Development

  • Improved software reliability and quality
  • Reduced development costs
  • Enhanced customer satisfaction
  • Better risk management
  • Increased understanding of the system

Failure mode analysis can be applied at any level in application design: architecture, application, data, network and/or hardware component level.

FMEA Process: Step-by-Step Guide

1. Define the Scope and Objectives

Clearly define the scope of the FMEA. What system, process, or component will be analyzed? What are the objectives?

  • List the functions of the system or process being analyzed. What is it supposed to do?
  • For each function, identify the ways in which it could fail. What could go wrong?
  • For each failure mode, identify the effects on the system, customer, and environment
  • Assign a severity rating (1-10 scale, where 1 is minor and 10 is catastrophic)

2. Identify Root Causes

For each failure mode, identify the root causes. Why would this failure occur?

  • Assign a likelihood rating (1-10 scale, where 1 is unlikely and 10 is very likely)
  • Identify any existing controls that prevent or detect failure modes
  • Assign a detection rating (1-10 scale, where 1 is easy to detect and 10 is very unlikely to detect)

3. Calculate RPN

Calculate the Risk Priority Number for each failure mode:

RPN = Severity √ó Likelihood √ó Detection

4. Prioritize and Act

Analyze the FMEA to identify the most critical priorities. Assign scores to each node, and focus on nodes with high scores.

Corrective Actions

For critical failure modes (high RPN), corrective action is urgent. After identifying nodes with high scores, publish an action plan and take corrective action.

Corrective actions can include:

Action TypeDescription
New alertsSet up alerts to notify the concerned team in case of failure
DR/BCP PlaybookCreate a playbook for disaster recovery and business continuity
Additional RunbooksCreate runbooks for the operations team to handle failures
Methodology UpdateUpdate the methodology to prevent future failures

Validating the Risk and Solution

After corrective action, validate the risk to ensure it has been reduced to an acceptable level.
